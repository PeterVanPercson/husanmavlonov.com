01Executive summary

defex builds Jetson-powered defect-inspection stations for foreign-invested SMEs in the Yangtze River Delta. v1 is a single-camera, single-defect-class station running on the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano Super: YOLOv8n inference via TensorRT FP16, hardware-triggered through GPIO, with Modbus TCP output to existing PLC infrastructure and sub-50 ms decision latency. The SKU is structured as OpEx — $5,000 upfront + $350/month — to slip inside plant-manager discretionary spend, bypassing the overseas-HQ capex cycles that kill the typical AOI vendor pitch.

The team is the Mavlonov twins. Husan (CEO) studies at Shanghai Jiao Tong University, has shipped a production Claude-API product (EduRadar), and runs a 1,500-member AI/hardware community (BuildCored). Hasan (CTO) studies at East China University of Science and Technology, was Class Leader for the AI '28 cohort, has published research endorsed for arXiv submission (StableMind), trained CNN classifiers at the University of Cambridge ML/NN program, and shipped production RFID hardware-integration backends at Tracky in Tashkent. Same family, same city, same building rhythm. Two engineers, one decision loop.

The wedge segment is rare in the literature but obvious on the ground. 太仓 (Taicang) hosts 560+ German firms; 嘉定 / 松江 / 安亭 host the bulk of Japanese auto-parts manufacturing in Shanghai. These factories share three properties absent from typical Chinese tier-2 suppliers: plant managers conduct business in English, procurement cycles run 6–8 weeks rather than 6–9 months, and capex limits for line-side improvements often sit with the local plant manager. The HSK 1 language gap that would block a foreign founder from the broader CN market stops being binding here. 信创 (domestic-substitution) policy aligns with our edge-only architecture: no cloud, no data egress.

02The YRD wedge

The cheap-AOI gap in the Yangtze River Delta is structural, not temporary. Cognex, Siemens, and Koh Young price their AOI systems at ¥200k+ (~$28K+), optimized for tier-1 EMS (Foxconn, Luxshare, BYD). Below that, the market is left on manual inspection — 3–8 inspectors per shift at ¥4–6k each per month, with escape rates that fail customer AQL audits and turnover that erodes consistency. Domestic challengers (微亿智造, 创新奇智, 思普锐) sit at ¥80–200k and target the same tier-1 segment. Nothing in the market hits the ¥35–70k OpEx tier where the foreign-invested SME plant manager has discretionary purchasing power.

The insight Foreign-invested SMEs in the YRD have English-fluent decision makers, 6–8 week procurement cycles, ¥30–50k plant-manager discretion, and AQL pressure baked into customer contracts. They're the perfect wedge — and they're invisible to everyone else.

The decision path — visualized

Critical path (green) must complete in < 50 ms per part. Non-blocking lane (gray) handles persistence, UI, and reports on a separate output queue — never in the decision path.

Non-negotiable architectural constraint · the 50 ms rule

Capture, inference, decision, and PLC I/O run on dedicated threads communicating through a bounded ring buffer. The decision thread never blocks on disk, network, or UI. The reject coil must fire within 50 ms of trigger or a defective part ships and we lose the contract. SQLite writes, log rotation, UI redraws, and dashboard polling are all consumers of a separate output queue — never in the decision path.

11Frequently asked

Why Jetson and not a Raspberry Pi or a Coral TPU?

Pi-class boards can't sustain p99 < 50 ms YOLOv8n at 5 MP with margin for retraining headroom. Coral USB Accelerators handle inference but force CPU bottlenecks on capture + pre-process. Jetson Orin Nano Super (40 TOPS · 8 GB) gives us 3× headroom on the latency budget and a full CUDA stack for OpenCV acceleration — meaning we ship one v1 architecture and don't re-platform for v2.

How does pricing actually clear the plant manager's signature?

Foreign-invested SMEs in the YRD typically authorize plant managers ¥30,000–¥50,000 ($4.2K–$7K) discretionary spend on line improvements without overseas HQ approval. Our $5K upfront fits inside that envelope. The $350/month service line is OpEx — pulled from the operations budget the plant manager already controls, not the capex budget that requires the 6–9 month HQ approval cycle that kills competitor pitches.

What happens to customer data? Does anything leave the factory?

Nothing. defex is edge-only by architecture. Inference, decision, and PLC I/O happen on the on-site Jetson. SQLite traceability and image archive write to NVMe locally. Daily reports are generated locally and emailed via the customer's SMTP if they enable it; otherwise sit on the device. This aligns with 信创 (domestic-substitution) policy and removes the data-egress conversation that blocks cloud-AI vendors.

What's the failure mode if your model misses a defect?

v1 ships configured for high recall (≥ 0.88). The trade-off is over-rejection (~5–8% false reject in the field), which is recoverable via human spot-check on the reject lane. This is the deliberately safer end of the precision/recall curve for first deployment — escaped defects cause AQL audit failures that lose contracts; over-rejects cost a few extra inspector minutes. We tune toward higher precision in v2 once we have 4+ weeks of customer-line data.

Why a twin team and not a solo founder?

Three reasons. (1) Trust starts at maximum — no equity haggling, no founder-dating, no misaligned commitment level. (2) Hardware-AI products have two distinct halves; one founder per half is the right structure. The CTO debugs sensors and trains models; the CEO closes plant managers and runs the application stack. (3) Coordination cost is family-low — standups happen at dinner. The CTO doesn't need a flight booked to fix a GPIO timing issue.

What about Chinese tier-1 manufacturing? Why not start there?

Tier-1 EMS (Foxconn, Luxshare, BYD) are already buying Cognex and Koh Young at ¥200k+. Their procurement is a 9–18 month bureaucracy. A foreign founder with no domestic deployment record can't credibly enter that pipeline. Foreign-invested SMEs are the structural wedge that gets us proof-of-deployment and a customer base; tier-2 Chinese suppliers become reachable in Year 3 once we have references.

What does "Z Fellows-backed" mean here?

Z Fellows is a small-grant + community program for founders in their teens and early twenties, accepting roughly 30 per cohort. Husan was accepted in early 2026. The capital is modest by Silicon Valley standards but the credentialing + the alumni network unlock investor conversations and customer warm intros that a 19-year-old solo founder couldn't otherwise reach.
